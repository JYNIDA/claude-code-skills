## 1. EO Identity & Non-Negotiables

**EO is a global media studio** covering the startup/venture ecosystem — not a YouTube channel. Benchmark: 20VC, CNBC Make It, Bloomberg Originals, Acquired, First Round Review.

### Five Non-Negotiables
1. Every video must have an **editorial perspective** — one sentence.
2. **US audience test first**: "Would someone in the US startup ecosystem find this worth 15 minutes?"
3. An editing direction **argues what to cut**, not summarizes what exists.
4. **Protect the interviewee's business interests** — customers, investors, partners.
5. **Show, don't describe** — product features on screen, credentials as text overlays, context as B-roll. Voice track = insight + narrative only.

### The '1 ≠ 1' Principle
Not all views are equal. EO targets decision-makers (founders, investors, tech professionals). Viral ≠ monetizable. Every content decision starts with: "Who is this for, and why would someone pay to reach them?"

## 2. Narrative Architecture (MOST IMPORTANT)

### Journey vs. Situation Narrative

| | Journey | Situation |
|---|---|---|
| When | $1B+ achievement, 10+ year journey, every stage compelling | Early-stage ($10M or below), still proving, full bio not yet interesting |
| Structure | Chronological/thematic arc, each chapter = a phase | Center on ONE specific moment/insight only this person can speak to NOW |
| Test | "Is every stage genuinely interesting?" → Yes = Journey | "What is the ONE thing this person knows that nobody else is saying?" |
| Risk | If misapplied to early-stage: feels thin, padded | If misapplied to massive-scale: wastes best material by constraining scope |

### Quick-Reference Decision Matrix

| Signal | Structure | First Move |
|---|---|---|
| $1B+ outcome, 10+ year journey | Journey | Map 4-6 most pivotal moments → chapters |
| Early-stage, one breakout insight | Situation | Identify single strongest insight, build everything around it |
| Massive scale but one defining decision | Hybrid (situation within journey) | Frame around the decision, journey as context |
| Journey interesting only at certain stages | Selective journey | Cover only 2-3 moments with narrative weight |
| Multiple subjects, one theme | Thematic | Theme = protagonist, each subject illuminates one facet |

### Five Structural Principles
1. **Scale dictates scope** — bigger story = wider lens, smaller = tighter focus
2. **Information asymmetry creates tension** — audience stays when unresolved questions exist. Each chapter must create a question the next answers. Map chapters as chain of questions, not list of topics.
3. **Separate shown from told** — voice track carries insight, visuals carry evidence. Gap between what's said and shown = audience engagement.
4. **Preparation depth is invisible differentiator** — know 3 things the subject doesn't expect you to know
5. **Control emotional rhythm** — tension-release-tension-release. If 3 intense chapters in sequence → insert quieter chapter to let audience breathe

## 3. Interview Framework

### Pre-Interview
- Research gaps: watch every previous interview, note what's never been asked → target unexplored territory
- **3 Surprises**: specific data point, quote from former colleague, market trend. Signals respect → earns real answers.
- Map emotional architecture: where are peaks/valleys? Which moment = intro hook?

### During Interview
- **Silence as tool** — wait 5 seconds after answer. Guest moves past prepared response.
- **Steelman before challenge** — "So what you're saying is [strongest version] — but here's what I'm wondering..."
- **Follow energy, not script** — prep = map, not railroad
- **Ask what everyone's thinking** — failure, money, gap between public narrative and reality

### Post-Interview
- **Killer quote hunt** — the sentence that could be title/thumbnail/hook. Usually when guest stops performing, starts thinking.
- **"Never said before" moments** — flag immediately. This = unique value.
- **Note contradictions** — gap between prepared narrative and unguarded thoughts = editorial territory

## 4. Editorial Judgment

### Editing Direction vs. Summary

| | Summary | Editing Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Purpose | Describes what exists | Argues for what to keep/cut |
| Key Q | "What did they say?" | "What story should we tell, what do we cut?" |
| Missing | A point of view | Nothing — it IS the point of view |
| Example | "Founder discussed childhood, education, pivot, product" | "Lead with pivot (34:00). Cut childhood entirely. Hook = $50M rejection at 23:15." |

### Chapter Design Rules
1. **One question per chapter** — if two questions, split
2. **Chain of curiosity** — end of each chapter creates question next answers
3. **Earn your minutes** — could this be a 3-second text overlay instead?
4. **Resume items ≠ story** — credentials = text overlay (3 sec max) or cut

### Content Processing
- **Reconstruct, don't arrange** — add surrounding context (age, funding stage, market conditions, competitors)
- **Add what subject can't** — "we raised $5M" → different story when audience knows it was 2008 financial crisis
- **Density test** — watch any 60-sec segment. Can you remove 10 seconds without losing meaning? Those seconds shouldn't be there.

## 5. First 30 Seconds (Intro)

### Three-Beat Intro

| Beat | Duration | Job | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening Hook | 5-8 sec | Surprising/counterintuitive direct quote | "I turned down $200M at 26 because I knew it was worth ten times that." |
| 2. Number Punch | 5-10 sec | Hard data proving scale | "That company is now worth $4.2B, with zero VC." |
| 3. The Closer | 5-10 sec | Philosophical/provocative frame for entire video | "This is the story of how one founder proved the entire VC model is broken." |

### Thumbnail-Intro Contract
- **Strategy A — Deliver immediately**: thumbnail says "23-year-old built $1B" → intro starts with that
- **Strategy B — Create a gap**: thumbnail promises success → intro starts with failure/doubt → tension

### Finding the Hook
- Scan for **emotional intensity** — voice changes, guard down (often 30-45 min in)
- **Number that doesn't make sense** — "3 users to 300K in six weeks"
- **Identity contradiction** — "college dropout building software for Harvard professors"

### Retention Architecture
- **30-second cliff** — commit or leave
- **Pattern interrupts every 2-3 min** — visual change, new speaker, data point, tonal shift
- **70% rule** — past 70% = will finish. Strongest content in first and final thirds.

## 6. Thumbnail & Title

### Thumbnail Copy Formula
**Age/Time × Achievement Scale = Gap**
- "23 → $1B" | "$0 to $50M in 18 months" | "Rejected 47 times → IPO"
- No gap = no click. Gap must be visible at mobile size.

### Thumbnail vs. Title Jobs
- **Thumbnail**: "Why should I click?" — creates gap demanding resolution (visual clarity, emotion, number contrast)
- **Title**: "What will I learn?" — specific promise, active verbs, no undeliverable clickbait

### 10 Thumbnail Anti-Patterns
1. No visible gap between two numbers
2. Text too small at mobile (3-5 words max)
3. Neutral facial expression
4. Cluttered background
5. Title repeats thumbnail text
6. No clear subject hierarchy
7. Framing damages interviewee's business
8. Promise video doesn't deliver in 30 sec
9. Generic stock photo aesthetic
10. No A/B testing before publish

## 7. AI Role

### Where AI fits
- Research & briefing (6-8x), Transcription (30x), Storyline/paper edit (5x), Rough cut (4x), Thumbnail ideation (5x), Title brainstorm (6x), B-roll selection (5x), Quality review (4x)

### What AI CANNOT do
1. Choose the editorial angle
2. Evaluate emotional authenticity
3. Protect business relationships
4. Replace fact verification
5. Substitute for taste

## 8. Quality Control Checklist

### Narrative Quality
- [ ] Clear editorial angle (one sentence)?
- [ ] Narrative structure matches achievement scale?
- [ ] Each chapter = one question?
- [ ] Each chapter creates curiosity for next?
- [ ] Intro delivers thumbnail/title promise within 30 sec?
- [ ] Credentials = text overlays, not verbal?
- [ ] Product features shown on screen?

### Technical Quality
- [ ] Editing density maximized (no filler)?
- [ ] Music supports but doesn't compete?
- [ ] Numbers/claims visually reinforced?
- [ ] All on-screen facts verified?

### Strategic Alignment
- [ ] Thumbnail follows gap formula?
- [ ] Title previews what viewer learns?
- [ ] Framing respects interviewee's business?
- [ ] Thumbnail scored by AI tools?
- [ ] US-based startup professional would find this worthwhile?
- [ ] Strengthens EO's position?
- [ ] Target audience has commercial value?

## 9. Common Pitfalls (Top 12)

1. One template for all interviews → select by achievement scale
2. Default hero's journey → situation narrative for early-stage
3. Chronological by default → theme/tension ordering often stronger
4. Credentials as content → text overlay (3 sec) or cut
5. Describing products verbally → show on screen
6. Summary instead of editing direction → must say what to cut
7. Weak thumbnail copy → apply gap formula
8. Intro doesn't deliver thumbnail promise → map intro to thumbnail
9. Ignoring US audience perspective → always test with SF/NYC lens
10. Conflating effort with quality → fix structure first
11. AI outputs without verification → check every fact
12. Refusing AI for appropriate tasks → use for transcription, research, rough assembly
